Why marathon pace is shown as a watch pace. Your goal is a chip time over the certified 42.195km, but a GPS watch reads about 1% long on a flat road marathon. If you run the naive chip pace (6:10/km) on the watch, you finish slow. So race pace is built slightly fast on purpose: 6:05-6:10/km on the watch delivers a 4:20 chip time. Run to the watch number.

Why no interval sessions? Marathon energy is almost entirely aerobic. Dedicated VO2 intervals carry a 48-72hr recovery cost that competes with the volume and threshold work that actually move a marathon. Your two 10K B-races supply all the fast stimulus you need early on, and strides keep the legs sharp at no recovery cost.

Long Run and MP Progression

Long runs step 10 → 15 → 17 → 19 → 22 → 24 → 30 (peak, Week 11), then 25, then taper. The 30km peak is deliberate: both your marathons fell apart at 30km, so you need to have run to and through that point in training, fully fuelled, before race day.

MP blocks respect the 40% week-on-week rule: 6km (Wk 8) → 8km (Wk 10) → 11km (Wk 11 peak) → 8km (Wk 13). The 11km peak sits inside the 30km long run.

Menstrual Cycle

You have flagged the menstrual phase as your hard window and you are tracking a regular 29-day cycle. The one weekly quality session is movable: if Friday's quality or a key long run lands on day 1-2 of your period and you feel flat or heavy, that is physiology, not weakness. Downgrade to easy and shift the quality by a day or two using the edit and drag tools. A regular cycle is also a useful signal that your energy intake is adequate (see Fuelling).

Fuelling is the headline of this block

You named fuelling as what has held you back, and both marathons confirmed it at 30km. Late-race collapse in a trained runner is almost always a fuelling failure, not a fitness failure. Fixing this is worth more minutes than any session in the plan.

This is not the block to run an energy deficit. You have said you are actively cutting. Under-eating during a marathon build works directly against your number one limiter: it lowers the glycogen you can store, blunts gut training, slows recovery, and raises injury risk. A regular period is a good sign your energy availability is currently adequate, keep it that way. Hold body-composition goals until after Abingdon, then resume. If your cycle becomes irregular during the block, treat that as a flag to eat more, not train more, and speak to your GP.

Daily Flags

  • Waking HR 5+ bpm above baseline, 2 mornings running: drop to easy, no quality.
  • Waking HR 8+ bpm above baseline: full rest, reassess next morning.
  • Any sharp or persistent pain (shin, calf, knee, foot): stop, ice, 2 days off if still there.
  • Under 5 hours sleep: downgrade quality to easy.
  • Day 1-2 of your period and feeling flat: downgrade and shift the quality, do not force it.

Session Signals

  • Easy pace dropping 15+ sec/km at the same HR over a week: fatigue building, deload or drop the next quality.
  • Cannot hold MP for the full block: recalibrate the target down a tier.
  • GI distress on a long run: back off the carb rate, do not abandon fuelling. Build it again gradually.
  • Peak 30km long run falls apart before 25km: set Goal B (4:30) as the plan and run it well.

One Quality Session Per Week

This block runs one quality session a week throughout. At your volume that is the right call: it protects recovery and lets the easy running do its aerobic job. A second weekly quality session is the natural next step in a future block, once you have completed a full cycle near 50km/week without breakdown.

Missed Sessions and Race Week Illness

Do not stack missed sessions. The next session is whatever was already planned, not the missed one bolted on.

Mild cold, head only, no fever: race goes ahead.

Chest, lungs or fever: do not race. There will be other marathons.
